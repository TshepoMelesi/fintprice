Components of Prices

Pricing is important, but knowing how to factor in components when calculating for pricing is more important. Everything that a business pays for to produce or sell a product should count as a pricing component.

Here are a few components needed to produce and sell a product:

Water & Electricity

As negligible as water and electricity may be in many businesses, it is important to factor them in. Because the businesses depend on them to either produce or sell products.

A product consuming 10 L of water and 5 kWh of electricity should account for them. If not, then the business will pay for these from its own profits.

Logisitics

Having to fetch materials by a vehicle, paying someone to deliver them to you or fetching them by a taxi is all forms of logistics. Every product should factor a portion of this transport cost. If not, the business will pay for them from its profits. Sometimes clients pay for logistics.

If 5 roses costs you to spend R500 for fetching them, then each rose will need to account for R100 of that transport cost. This is beside the delivery costs!

Materials | Ingridients

Though tricky, but if you track down quantities of material you need to produce a single product, then it will get easy for each product to account for the cost of materials it took to produce it.

R500 worth of wood (makes 5 chairs), R240 worth of wood glue (makes 20 chairs) and R200 worth of screws (makes 10 chairs), means that each chair would account for R100 of wood, R12 of wood glue and R20 of screws. In total, each chair will account for R132 of materials.

Salary | Wage | Commision

Business money is not your money. Instead of using business money for personal needs, set out your salary (and salaries of others), calculate this salary down to a single product. Every product should pay you a portion of what ends up making your salary (weekly, monthly, whatever suits you)

This may be time based or product based. Each bottle of water can pay you R1 or each table you resell could pay you R5. Each custom sketch art could pay you R70 per hour. Either way, your salary will be an expense of its own.

Tools and Machinery

Machines breaks, wear out and get stolen all the time. But every business account for this differently. Some get Insurance for their machines in case they get stolen. But wear outs are common too. Each product should account for the depreciation of machines used to produce or sell it.

A ice cream machine may cost R10000. It may be expected to run and make 100000 ice creams in its lifespan. That means, each ice cream should account for R0.10. This way, when the machine wears out, a new machine can be bought, not from business' profits. But from those R0.10. However it is not this easy to determine this amount. In some instances, you can look at the warranty of the machine. If it is 5 years, then, you can estimate how many ice creams you will sell in that 5 years.

Others

Beside direct expenses, there are indirect expenses. Accounting for indirect expenses is not straight forward. Sometimes, some of these indirect expenses are what the business pays for from its revenue or money.

Calling clients who placed orders with you is an expense, but it will be hard to associate that expense with a specific product. Marketing your products on social media is an expense too. Setting up brand identity is an expense too. All of these things needs to be paid for. But how or by what? Can products account for these? It is not clear enough.

Price Models

Pricing models have been studied and experimented in many and different business settings. Each of them with their advantages and disadvantages, this is why it is important to learn more about them before you conclude on a suitable model for your business.

Cost-Plus Model

This method uses a simple approach, price of a product is the sum of the cost of producing it plus profit margin.

It is the most common model amongst businesses, and FintPrice uses it on default.

Value-Based Model

Some products sell well when sold with value in mind. This is where the value based model comes in. However costs and margin are taken into consideration, then value pricing is added

This is mostly common in service businesses, also for intangible products.

Competitive Model

In some instances, competition determines a price range for what a business in that competition might sell their products at. It is common in markets with fewer competitors.

This is mostly common in telecom, banking and insurance businesses.

Freemium Model

In some odd cases, a business would sell a portion of their product for free, limited by use or timeframe.

This is mostly common in software businesses

Combination Model

Some businesses combine some of these models and make up a model that works well for their products and services.

This is mostly common in businesses with a range of products and services. This is because many services dont follow standards in terms of pricing and profiting from them. At the center of this is business sustainability.
